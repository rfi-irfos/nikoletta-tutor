What this is about

Grammar is only half the picture.

Language teaching has always been brilliant at one thing: grammar and vocabulary. Rules, structures, word lists, tests. And for many students, that is enough.

But there is a whole group of learners for whom it is not. Students who studied for years, know the rules, passed the tests, and still go completely silent the moment they have to actually speak. Not because the knowledge is missing. Because something else is in the way.

That something else is what this methodology addresses. Confidence. Nervous system regulation. The relationship between the student and the language itself.

The method

What it is built on.

The core idea

Most students who struggle to speak do not lack knowledge. They lack safety. When a student anticipates judgment, the nervous system activates a defensive state. In that state, even language the student genuinely knows becomes hard to reach. This is not a motivation problem. It is a physiological response.

1

Safety before content

Before any language task, the student needs to feel that mistakes are not dangerous here. Genuine interest in who the student is as a person, before asking them to perform in the language.

2

The person is the curriculum

The student's own interests, work, and real-life situations become the material. When content is personally meaningful, acquisition happens faster and sticks longer.

3

The planned dynamic

Sessions open with free, low-pressure conversation. Grammar emerges from what the student actually says, addressed just-in-time, then immediately applied in a real communicative context.

4

Visible belief

Genuine, visible excitement at a student's progress gives them direct evidence against the belief that they cannot do this. Small wins are made into events.

Grounded in
Polyvagal Theory (Porges) Affective Filter (Krashen) Person-Centered Approach (Rogers) TBLT (Prabhu) ZPD (Vygotsky) Self-Efficacy (Bandura)

How to participate

Five things to try.

Each principle works in both private sessions and group settings. Click any to see how to apply it.

1
Ask the student how they want to be taught

Before any content, find out what has not worked for this person before and what a good session feels like for them. Most teachers never ask this.

Ask directly in the first session: "What has not worked for you before?" and "What would a session look like where you leave feeling good?" Let them talk.

Structured
Add to your intake form

Include these two questions as required fields. Read answers before planning anything.

Intuitive
Ask it in conversation

Bring it up in the first five minutes. No clipboard. Let them talk and listen more than you speak.

Visual
Write it where you can see it

Note their answers on a sticky note and keep it visible during early sessions.

Digital
Let them type their own answers

Open a shared doc. Seeing their own words on screen often makes students more honest.

Anonymous written exercise in the first lesson. Two sentences: one thing that has never helped them in language class, and one thing they wish a lesson felt like.

Structured

Half-page form, first lesson plan. Read overnight, adapt next session.

Intuitive

Verbal round: each student says one word that describes their relationship with this language right now.

Visual

Two columns on the board, "what has not worked" and "what I wish for." Fill together.

Digital

Google Form for anonymous input. Students often more honest in writing.

2
Build one session around something they mentioned casually

Something they said without thinking — a show, a hobby, a trip they mentioned — becomes the material for an entire session. They did not know you were listening that closely. That is the point.

Pick something they mentioned last session that had energy behind it. Build the whole session around it without telling them in advance.

Structured
Log one detail after every session

Write down one thing they mentioned with energy. Review before planning the next session.

Intuitive
Open with it, unannounced

No prep note needed. Start the session around what they said and watch their face.

Visual
Three-column template

What they mentioned / how to use it / what language comes from it.

Digital
Open the session with a surprise doc

Build a shared slide around their interest and open it at the start without explanation.

At the end of one lesson, ask every student to write down one thing they are into right now. Collect the answers. Build the next lesson around the most common theme.

Structured

Use answers to plan a themed lesson for the following week.

Intuitive

Pick the one topic that gives you the most ideas and build freely.

Visual

Word cloud from their answers shown at the start of the next lesson as a hook.

Digital

Shared padlet so students can also see each other's answers.

3
Respond to a breakthrough with full, visible enthusiasm

When a student does something they have struggled with before, stop. Name it. Make it an event. The moment your reaction tells them it counted is the moment they start to believe they can do this.

You have the luxury of full attention. When the moment happens, pause. Say exactly what you noticed. Stay with it for a few seconds before moving on.

Natural
Start with the pause

Two seconds of silence signals something happened before you say a word.

Reserved
Name it specifically and calmly

"I want you to notice what you just did." A precise observation lands just as hard.

Online
Write it in the chat too

Say it and type it. Written words feel more permanent.

Young learners
Mark the moment physically

A high five, a point on a scoreboard. Physicality makes it stick.

In a group, praise specific enough to feel real but framed to include everyone.

Inclusive

"Did everyone hear that? That is exactly what fluent sounds like."

Visual

Keep a "moments" corner on the board. Write what the student said, word for word.

Young learners

A class "win wall" — physical or digital — where breakthroughs get posted.

Adults

"Hold on, say that again" followed by genuine group attention is often more powerful than praise.

4
Have a pivot plan ready and use it deliberately

You always have a backup prepared for this specific student or group. Not a generic easy task but something you know they enjoy. When the energy drops, you switch. You do not wait.

Before each session, decide what your pivot is. Have it ready before you start, not during.

Structured
Add a "pivot option" row to every plan

Fill it in before the session starts. When the moment comes, the decision is already made.

Intuitive
Keep a short list per student

Three or four things this student reliably responds well to. Pull from the list without overthinking.

Visual
Two colored sticky notes

One for the main plan, one for the pivot. Separate physically makes switching feel intentional.

Online
Pre-load the pivot in a tab

Keep pivot material open in a browser tab before the session. One click, not a scramble.

One student tired is manageable. Half the class flat means you need to move, and move before it bottoms out.

Structured

Prepare a "lazy lesson" folder with activities you know this group enjoys, ready to pull from at any point.

Intuitive

Read the room at the fifteen-minute mark. If energy is already low, switch then.

Visual

A physical card on your desk reminding you the pivot option exists. Removes hesitation.

School teachers

Frame the pivot as part of your lesson plan, not a deviation. Responsive teaching is pedagogy.

5
Track confidence and accuracy separately

Accuracy is how correct the language is. Confidence is how freely and willingly the student speaks. These are two different things and they move at different rates. The hypothesis: confidence often grows before accuracy does.

After each session, two private scores from 1 to 5: one for accuracy, one for how freely they spoke. Write both down with one sentence of context. Every session for the testing period.

Structured
Spreadsheet: date, accuracy, confidence

Fill within five minutes of the session ending while your impression is fresh.

Intuitive
Voice note right after the session

Say both scores out loud and one thing you noticed. Transcribe later.

Visual
Plot both scores on a chart

After four sessions, seeing the two lines diverge is often the most convincing moment.

Minimal
Track just one student

Two scores per session, four to six sessions. That is enough to see whether something is happening.

Pick two or three students you find most interesting. Observe the rest more loosely. Focus especially on the ones who are usually silent.

Structured

Simple grid for each selected student: session dates, accuracy note, confidence note after each lesson.

Intuitive

After each lesson: three names and one word for each — how freely did they speak today compared to last time?

Visual

Two colored dots per student per session. One color per dimension. Patterns become visible fast.

Large classes

Focus only on the students who are silent or rarely speak. That is where change will be most visible.

What to send me afterward

No template. Just honest observations.

  • What did you notice? What shifted, even slightly?
  • What surprised you?
  • What did not work, or felt unnatural to apply?
